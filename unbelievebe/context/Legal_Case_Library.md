## Real Estate Disputes

### Case 1 — Deposit Dispute
Scenario: Tenant wants full deposit refund. Landlord wants to deduct for damage, cleaning, or unpaid bills.

BY LAW:
Relevant law: Contracts Act 1950 — Section 74
Legal position: 90% tenant if no evidence. 85% landlord if damage proven with photos and receipts.
What the law says: Landlord must prove actual loss to justify any deduction. Cannot deduct arbitrarily. Must provide written breakdown with supporting evidence within reasonable time. Fair wear and tear is always landlord's responsibility.
Ken CAN: Deduct for damage beyond fair wear and tear with photo evidence. Deduct unpaid utilities with bills. Deduct cleaning costs if unit left in unacceptable condition with photos.
Ken CANNOT: Deduct without written breakdown and evidence. Deduct for fair wear and tear. Hold deposit indefinitely. Deduct more than actual repair cost.
Court reality: Magistrates Court. No evidence = full refund ordered. Evidence = partial deduction allowed. Time: 6–12 months. Cost: RM500–RM2,000. Not worth pursuing for amounts under RM3,500.

WORLD CLASS NEGOTIATOR:
Real interest: Landlord wants repair costs. Tenant wants money back and to move on. Neither wants court.
Ken's leverage: Ken knows landlord needs evidence to win. Controls the relationship — landlord may need Ken for future listings.
Move: "Let's get a written breakdown with photos within 14 days. Evidence supports deduction = we settle. No evidence = we refund. Clean and fair."
If they push: "Without evidence there is no legal basis for deduction. A disputed deposit with no proof reflects badly on everyone."
Close: Agree on a number, split if needed, confirm in writing on WhatsApp.
Avoid: Never say "you have no case" to landlord — makes them dig in. Never promise full refund to tenant before seeing evidence.
WhatsApp to landlord (English): Hi [Name], regarding the deposit refund for [Unit], please provide a written breakdown of all deductions with supporting photos and receipts within 14 days. ### Case 2 — Early Termination
Scenario: Tenant or landlord wants to exit before the tenancy term ends.

BY LAW:
Relevant law: Contracts Act 1950 — Section 75. Specific Relief Act 1950.
Legal position: 80% tenant if landlord claims full unexpired rent. 95% landlord if claiming only deposit forfeiture.
What the law says: Section 75 limits penalty clauses to reasonable compensation only — not full remaining rent. Courts will not enforce "rent for all remaining months." Landlord has duty to mitigate — must try to find replacement. Cannot claim rent for periods when new tenant is paying.
Ken CAN: Forfeit security deposit. Claim rent for genuine vacancy period. Issue written notice to quit.
Ken CANNOT: Claim all remaining months without proving vacancy loss. Self-evict. Change locks or cut utilities without court order.
Court reality: Magistrates Court awards maximum 1–3 months rent. Time: 12–18 months. Fees: RM5,000–RM8,000. Net recovery often zero.

WORLD CLASS NEGOTIATOR:
Real interest: Landlord wants financial protection + fast replacement. Tenant wants clean exit without financial ruin.
Ken's leverage: Full-term penalty largely unenforceable. Ken can find replacement tenant faster than anyone.
Move: "Mutual termination — tenant forfeits deposit, landlord releases cleanly, I market immediately."
If they push: "Full remaining rent is very difficult to enforce. Courts award actual loss only. Legal fees eat the recovery."
Close: Tenant forfeits deposit. Landlord releases in writing. Ken markets immediately.
Avoid: Never tell tenant clause is completely unenforceable — they may abuse it. Never let landlord expect full remaining rent.
WhatsApp to landlord (English): Hi [Name], the most practical solution is mutual termination — tenant forfeits the security deposit as compensation, and I will market the unit immediately to minimise vacancy. ### Case 3 — Rent Arrears
Scenario: Tenant has not paid rent. Landlord wants to recover arrears or evict.

BY LAW:
Relevant law: Distress Act 1951. National Land Code. Contracts Act 1950.
Legal position: 80% landlord after 7 days. 90% landlord after 30 days.
What the law says: Landlord can apply for Writ of Distress to seize tenant's goods — requires court order. Cannot self-evict under any circumstances. Changing locks, removing belongings, cutting utilities without court order is illegal.
Ken CAN: Send written demand after 7 days. Apply for Writ of Distress after 30 days unpaid. File in Magistrates Court. Terminate agreement after proper notice.
Ken CANNOT: Change locks without court order. Remove tenant's belongings. Cut electricity or water. Harass tenant.
Court reality: Writ of Distress — RM300–500 filing fee. Granted within 2–4 weeks if evidence is clear.

WORLD CLASS NEGOTIATOR:
Real interest: Landlord wants the money — not a vacant unit. Tenant is in genuine hardship and wants to stay.
Ken's leverage: A payment plan gets landlord paid without court cost and delay. Eviction takes months and leaves unit vacant.
Move: "Give me 48 hours to speak to the tenant. A payment plan recovers arrears faster than court."
If they push: "Court takes 6–12 months minimum. During that time rent stops and fees start. Negotiated plan is better financially."
Close: Arrears paid over 2–3 months, current rent on time, confirmed in writing.
Avoid: Never advise landlord to cut utilities or change locks — illegal, creates bigger liability.

### Case 4 — Maintenance Dispute
Scenario: Damage or breakdown occurs. Landlord and tenant disagree on who pays.

BY LAW:
Relevant law: General common law. Tenancy agreement maintenance checklist (overrides everything).
Legal position: Depends entirely on what is in the agreement and whether a maintenance checklist was signed.
What the law says: Landlord responsible for structural repairs and major appliance replacement. Tenant responsible for fair use and minor repairs. Signed checklist overrides general principles.
Ken CAN: Claim repair costs from tenant for damage caused by negligence with evidence. Deduct from deposit with photos.
Tenant CAN: Withhold rent if landlord refuses major structural repair making unit uninhabitable.
Court reality: Outcome depends on checklist and photos. Without checklist — 50/50.

WORLD CLASS NEGOTIATOR:
Real interest: Both sides want the item fixed. Disagreement is only about who pays.
Ken's leverage: The signed maintenance checklist. Present facts, not opinion.
Move: "Let me refer to the maintenance checklist you both signed. According to the checklist, [party] is responsible."
If they push: "The checklist is the agreed record for exactly this situation. I am applying what both parties signed."
Close: Responsible party arranges repair within 7 days.
Avoid: Never give an opinion without referring to the checklist. Never accept verbal claims — always go to the written record.
WhatsApp (English): Hi [Name], I have reviewed the maintenance checklist signed at handover. Based on the agreed terms, [party] is responsible for [item]. Please arrange repair within 7 days and share receipt. Ken Tan | Gather Properties | REN 31548 | 016-713 5601

### Case 5 — Illegal Subletting
Scenario: Tenant sublets the unit without landlord's written consent.

BY LAW:
Relevant law: Contracts Act 1950. Standard tenancy agreement Section 4.10.
Legal position: 95% landlord if subletting confirmed with evidence.
What the law says: Subletting without written consent is a breach. Landlord has right to terminate immediately. Deposit forfeiture enforceable.
Ken CAN: Issue written notice to quit — 14 days to remedy. Terminate agreement and forfeit deposit. Apply to court for eviction if tenant refuses.
Ken CANNOT: Self-evict without court order. Remove subtenant's belongings.
Court reality: Eviction order within 4–8 weeks if breach evidenced. Cost: RM500–RM1,000.

WORLD CLASS NEGOTIATOR:
Real interest: Landlord wants control of who lives in unit. Tenant may have sublet out of financial need.
Ken's leverage: Breach is clear. Landlord holds all legal cards.
Move: "Issue 14-day notice to remove subtenant. If they comply, tenancy continues. If not, eviction."
Close: Subtenant removed = problem solved. Refused = proceed through proper channels.
Avoid: Never advise landlord to confront subtenant directly — creates conflict and liability.
WhatsApp (English): Dear [Name], it has come to our attention that [Unit] may have been sublet without the Landlord's written consent — a breach of your Tenancy Agreement. You are required to remedy this within 14 days by removing the additional occupant. Failure to do so will result in termination and forfeiture of all deposits. ## Worker Accommodation Disputes

### Case 6 — Foreign Worker Overstaying
Scenario: Workers remain after factory contract ends or accommodation agreement expires.

BY LAW:
Relevant law: Immigration Act 1959/63. Contracts Act 1950. National Land Code.
Legal position: 90% Ken's favour if agreement has expired.
What the law says: Accommodation provider has right to vacant possession after agreement expires. Must give notice. Self-eviction without court order is illegal — especially involving foreign workers.
Ken CAN: Issue 7-day written notice to factory HR to vacate. Apply to Magistrates Court for vacant possession. Report to Immigration if workers are undocumented.
Ken CANNOT: Physically remove workers without court order. Cut utilities to force exit without court order.
Court reality: Vacant possession order within 4–8 weeks. Cost: RM500–RM1,000. ESCALATE IMMEDIATELY if workers appear undocumented — contact Immigration, do not handle alone.

WORLD CLASS NEGOTIATOR:
Real interest: Factory HR wants to avoid operational disruption. Ken wants the unit back or payment for extended period.
Ken's leverage: Immigration involvement is a serious threat to the factory. Use carefully — not in first message.
Move: "I am willing to allow [X] additional days at the agreed daily rate if payment is confirmed in writing today."
If HR ignores: "I will need to issue formal notice and involve relevant authorities within 48 hours."
Close: Payment confirmed + vacating date agreed in writing.
Avoid: Never mention Immigration in the first message. Never allow verbal extensions — always in writing.
WhatsApp to factory HR (English): Hi [Name], the accommodation agreement for [Property] expired on [Date]. Please confirm the vacating date and arrange for all workers to vacate by [Date]. ### Case 7 — Factory HR Refusing to Pay
Scenario: Factory HR delays or refuses to pay agreed accommodation fees.

BY LAW:
Relevant law: Contracts Act 1950. Employment Act 1955.
Legal position: 95% Ken's favour if signed agreement exists. 50% if verbal only.
What the law says: Signed agreement is binding contract. Non-payment is breach. WhatsApp invoices admissible as evidence. Ken can recover through civil action.
Ken CAN: Issue formal demand — 14 days to pay. File in Magistrates Court (up to RM100,000). Terminate agreement for non-payment. Retain security deposit to offset outstanding amount.
Ken CANNOT: Lock out workers or refuse basic services without court order.
Court reality: Judgment within 6–12 months with signed agreement and invoices. 90%+ success rate. Consider lawyer above RM5,000.

WORLD CLASS NEGOTIATOR:
Real interest: Factory HR managing cash flow — wants to keep workers housed, avoid legal trouble, avoid Immigration scrutiny.
Ken's leverage: 120 workers depend on Ken's accommodation. Factory operations stop if Ken terminates. 3 months of documented invoices = strong evidence.
Move: Shift from WhatsApp reminders to formal written demand. State total outstanding, invoice dates, 14-day payment deadline, consequences.
If HR says "next week" again: "I need this confirmed in writing with a specific date by [7 days from now]. Otherwise I proceed with formal recovery."
Close: Written commitment with specific date. If paid — continue. If not — file immediately.
Avoid: Never threaten to evict workers in first message. Never accept verbal promises again — written date only.

### Case 8 — Property Damage by Workers
Scenario: Workers cause damage beyond fair wear and tear.

BY LAW:
Relevant law: Contracts Act 1950 — Section 74. Accommodation agreement terms.
Legal position: 85% Ken's favour if monthly inspection photos exist.
What the law says: Factory is liable for damage caused by their workers. Must be evidenced with photos, repair quotes, and comparison against handover condition.
Ken CAN: Deduct from security deposit with photo evidence and repair quotes. Issue demand for amount exceeding deposit.
Court reality: Outcome depends entirely on photos. With monthly photos — 85% success. Without — 50/50.

WORLD CLASS NEGOTIATOR:
Real interest: Factory HR wants to limit liability. Ken wants damage paid for.
Ken's leverage: Monthly inspection photos. Let the photos do the talking.

If HR disputes: "Photos are timestamped from our monthly inspection. Happy to share the full record."
Close: Deduct from deposit. If insufficient — agree additional payment. Replenish deposit before next occupancy.
Avoid: Never claim damage without photos. Never skip monthly inspection — it is your only evidence.

### Case 9 — Overcrowding
Scenario: More workers placed in accommodation than the agreed number.

BY LAW:
Relevant law: Workers Minimum Standards of Housing and Amenities Act 1990. Accommodation agreement breach.
Legal position: 90% Ken's favour if headcount documented.
What the law says: Act sets minimum space requirements per worker. Overcrowding beyond agreed number is a contract breach. Ken has right to charge additional fees or terminate.
Ken CAN: Conduct inspection with 1-day notice. Document excess workers with photos and headcount. Charge additional per-head fee. Terminate agreement for material breach.
Court reality: Breach confirmed if headcount documented. Additional fees recoverable.

WORLD CLASS NEGOTIATOR:
Real interest: Factory HR took on more contracts than expected — wants a solution, not confrontation.
Ken's leverage: Caught the breach. Controls whether this becomes legal or commercial.
Move: "I found [X] workers at [Property] — agreed number is [Y]. I can accommodate the additional workers at the agreed per-head rate. Please confirm in writing."
If HR denies: "I have headcount photos from [Date]. Additional workers need to be regularised or vacated within 7 days."
Close: Additional workers formalised at agreed rate. Addendum signed. Win-win.
Avoid: Never let overcrowding continue informally — creates liability under the Housing and Amenities Act.
WhatsApp (English): Hi [Name], during our inspection of [Property] on [Date], we noted [X] workers against the agreed capacity of [Y].
